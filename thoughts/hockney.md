## David Hockney
<small>{{ page.date | date: "%B %Y" }}</small>

The Industrial Revolution transformed thermal energy into mechanical energy, and today, electrical energy can directly generate intelligence. Will human intellect still hold value in the future? I recently came across David Hockney, who pondered a similar question within the realm of painting.

In an era where photography became widespread, Hockney fell into deep self-doubt, unable to find the meaning of "easel realism." To find theoretical support for his instinctual desire to paint, Hockney embarked on a long-term exploration and made two major discoveries that resolved his inner conflict:

1. Through photographic collages, he realized that a camera captures a single instant from a fixed perspective, whereas human drawing is a "synthesis of time and space" achieved by repeatedly looking up to observe. This discovery established that painting from life (human subjective observation) holds an irreplaceable value that cameras cannot replicate.

2. Through his research, he confirmed that classical realist masters since the Western Renaissance (such as Ingres) widely used optical lenses (like the camera obscura) to trace their images. This finding shattered his insurmountable reverence for classical realism, proving that painting "as accurately as a photograph" actually violates the natural laws of naked-eye observation.

![Secret Knowledge](/images/thoughts/secret.jpg)

On the title page of his book, [Secret Knowledge: Rediscovering the Lost Techniques of the Old Masters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hockney%E2%80%93Falco_thesis), he placed two images: on the left, a medieval painting from before humanity mastered perspective techniques, and on the right, a work by Van Gogh.

After shedding his psychological baggage, Hockney held Van Gogh in the highest esteem. He realized that Van Gogh's clearly visible brushstrokes and rapid capture of subjective impressions on site represented a truly honest form of painting that belongs to human naked-eye observation. In his 70s, Hockney was introduced to the iPad and fell in love with the tool. In his later years, he let go of the burden of being a "master"; his style became as simple, natural, and joyful as a child's, reaching the realm of what the ancient Chinese described as "plain and innocent" (平淡天真).
